Studio Ghibli Films

Delving in

I am dedicating this page to Princess Mononoke. This film has always struck a chord in me. Perhaps it's the nostalgia taking over as I remember watching this film for the first time at the age of 8 years old. I had grown up watching some of Hayao Miyazaki's other works and expected another magical but light-hearted adventure catered to a younger audience. I was mistaken in the best way possible. Princess Mononoke's theme is that of such which reflects a deeper understanding of humanity and nature. Encapsulating beauty with the real world elements, this easily became not only my favorite Studio Ghibli film, but also my favorite film of all time.

Animated by Studio Ghibli and directed by Hayao Miyazaki, Princess Mononoke was released July 12, 1997. Standing alongside many of their Japanese animated films, it is the 10th that tells of an epic historical tale highlighting the battle between man and nature and progress against the environment. It became the highest-grossing film in Japan of 1997 winning Best Japanese Movie, Best Animation and Japanese Movie Fans' Choice awards during the 52nd Mainichi Film Awards.

Storyline

The storyline follows Ashitaka, a young prince, who must travel far to the west leaving behind his village in hopes to search for a cure. A curse brought upon him by that of Nago, the demon boar god, grants him superhuman strength at the cost of siphoning his life away. The journey to the west from where the demon boar god seems to have originated leads him into an encounter between the gods of a forest and humans, the inhabitants of Iron Town. The gods intend to protect their forest while the humans intend to utilize the resources of the forest at whatever cost for the advancement of their town. Ashitaka with eyes not blinded by hate sees to put an end to this deadly struggle between man and nature which has been going on for ages.

Characters

Ashitaka

Ashitaka, the main protagonist, is a prince of the Emishi. He is depicted as a fearless warrior. Skilled, yet gentle and curious and guided by eyes unclouded by hate. With these traits, he was meant to become the leader of his tribe but upon rescuing his village from Nago, the demon boar god, Ashitaka's arm becomes afflicted with a curse that will eventually kill him. Sent westward by the village shaman, Ashitaka searches for the cause of Nago's corruption and a cure for his curse. To leave the village and tribe however, means to never be able to return again, thus Ashitaka becomes exiled. Upon arriving at Irontown, Ashitaka finds himself caught between the inhabitants waging war on the forest and the forest gods. Amidst the battle, Ashitaka meets San, human but daughter raised of the wolf tribe and wolf goddess Moro. As he becomes more entangled in San's life and the conflict, he knows for certain that he must instill in everyone to walk the path of harmony.

Ashitaka is not a cheerful, worry-free boy. He is a melancholy boy who has a fate. I feel that I am that way myself, but until now, I have not made a film with such a character. — Hayao Miyazaki

San

San, the heroine of the film, is referred to as the warrior-princess of the wolf gods. She is depicted as stubborn and short-tempered yet courageous. Her parents were found defiling the forest which provoked the wolf goddess Moro to attack them. Her parents, while holding San, threw her to Moro as a sacrifice to save their own lives. Contrary to eating San, Moro instead raised her as her own daughter, and in turn, San treats Moro as her mother. San's aims to protect the forest as she rejects her own humanity and even thinks of herself as a wolf. She has attempted to assassinate Eboshi, the leader of Irontown numerous times before Ashitaka's arrival. San believes that the end of Eboshi means the end of the humans and their defiling of the forest. San slowly acknowledges her human side as Ashitaka shows her the perspecive of Iron Town's inhabitants. She too must choose to decide the fate of the forest and the humans.

I'm not afraid to die! I'd do anything to get you humans out of my forest! —San to Ashitaka

Meaning

Movie trailer

Without spoiling the film, I wanted to share some of the main takeaways as said by Miyazaki himself:

In the past, humans hesitated when they took lives, even non-human lives. But society had changed, and they no longer felt that way. As humans grew stronger, I think that we became quite arrogant, losing the sorrow of "we have no other choice." I think that in the essence of human civilization, we have the desire to become rich without limit, by taking the lives of other creatures. And it leads to the idea that the world is not just for humans, but for all life, and humans are allowed to live in a corner of the world. It's not like we can coexist with nature as long as we live humbly, and we destroy it because we become greedy. When we recognize that even living humbly destroys nature, we don't know what to do. And I think that unless we put ourselves in the place where we don't know what to do and start from there, we cannot think about environmental issues or issues concerning nature. — Hayao Miyazaki
